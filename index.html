Chronology of a Collapse

Trace the 4-year macro arc captured in Sorkin's narrative, from speculative euphoria to legislative reckoning.

Feb 1, 1929

The Warning Shots

The Federal Reserve wrestles internally with runaway speculative momentum. Prominent banks continue pouring liquidity into the call money market, financing stock purchases on razor-thin 10% margins.

Sep 3, 1929

The Zenith

The Dow Jones Industrial Average scales an unprecedented closing peak of 381.17. The widespread psychological assumption across Wall Street remains unified: structural economic forces have permanently shifted upward.

Oct 24, 1929

Black Thursday

Panic ignites on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange. Volume shatters historic baselines. A midday consortium of institutional bankers led by Thomas Lamont orchestrates a massive financial backstop to restore order, providing temporary stabilization.

Oct 29, 1929

Black Tuesday

The system fractures comprehensively. Panic selling overwhelms the ticker tapes. Fortunes evaporate instantly as massive blocks of stock are liquidated blindly, finalizing the end of the roaring bull market.

Jun 31, 1933

The Aftermath & Recovery

The baseline parameters of economic reality are fundamentally reshaped. Markets begin scraping a true floor as structural federal legislative and monetary interventions slowly shift the economy into a painful recovery era.

Literary Critique & Perspectives

The Human Drama of Leverage

Sorkin translates what could easily be a clinical, macroeconomic autopsy into an intense psychological character thriller. Much like his approach in Too Big to Fail, the ultimate value of 1929 is its granular deconstruction of choice under pressure. By exploring unpublished letters, meeting transcripts, and diaries, he proves that financial systems are fundamentally run on emotion, hubris, and fragile social psychology.

"Sorkin delivers a people's tragedy told through the lens of the leading players—an immersive, forensic blueprint for understanding the cycles of human speculation."

While economic purists might find the deep micro-focus on specific banking suites light on deep regulatory policy theory, the book shines in tracing the terrifyingly familiar human behaviors that drive asset bubbles across generations.
